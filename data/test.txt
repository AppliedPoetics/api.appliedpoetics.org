Gerald was a particular kind of duck — not in his plumage, which was an ordinary speckled brown, and not in his waddle, which was unremarkable even by duck standards. Gerald was particular in what he loved: reflections.
It started on a Tuesday in early spring. Gerald was drifting across Miller's Pond when he noticed the water had gone perfectly still, and there, floating beneath him, was another duck — mirror-perfect, moving when he moved, tilting when he tilted. He spent the entire morning bowing and spinning, watching his double bow and spin back.
From that day on, Gerald collected them.
A rain puddle in the gravel path held a tiny, wobbling sky — he visited it every morning until it dried up. The side of a chrome milk canister outside the old Hargrove farm gave him seven distorted Geralds at once, each one funnier than the last. On cold November mornings, he'd find himself in the black ice at the pond's edge, crisp and perfect as a photograph.
The other ducks thought he was odd. Meredith, who lived under the willow and had opinions about everything, said he wasted too much time staring at water. "There are seeds," she reminded him, "on the east bank."
Gerald thanked her and went to look at his reflection in her eyes.
Winter came hard that year. The pond froze solid, the puddles disappeared, and the milk canister was moved inside the barn. Gerald found himself with nothing to collect. He sat on the ice and felt the cold come up through his feet.
That was when he noticed the sky.
It was enormous — gray and lavender and, near the horizon, a burning, impossible amber. And it was moving, rolling with clouds, flickering with the last light of an afternoon in December. He'd always had the sky in his reflections, but only ever looked down at it.
Now he looked up.
It turned out the sky was the best reflection of all — vast and impermanent, different every single morning, impossible to collect. You could only ever catch it in the moment you were standing in, which Gerald slowly understood was the whole point.
He still visited puddles in the spring. He still admired the chrome canister. But every evening now, Gerald would tip his brown head back and watch the light change, free and fleetingly his.
Meredith noticed, eventually.
"What are you doing?" she asked one evening, following his gaze upward.
"Collecting," said Gerald.
She looked at the sky for a long moment. "Hm," she said, and didn't go back to the east bank for almost an hour.
